Improving Transportation Safety in Atlanta

Authors: Jackson Simonson, Ryan Zhang, Pranav Devarinti, Wilson Chen
To: Atlanta Department of Transportation

The Problem: Micromobility Risks & Failed Solutions

Improving transportation safety in Atlanta requires a specific focus on roads used by diverse modes of transit. Previous efforts to improve safety on major roads like Marietta Street have yielded poor results. A recent evaluation of street redesigns found that projects lacking physical separation failed to produce meaningful safety improvements for Light Individual Transport (LIT+) users (Wallace et al., 2021). In fact, accident rates for these vulnerable groups often increased post-project because the designs failed to address high vehicle speeds and complex intersection conflicts.

The current state of Marietta Street creates a hostile environment where delivery trucks frequently block painted bike lanes, forcing riders into 45mph traffic. Unclear signage and the lack of vertical barriers create confusion, leading to dangerous merging behaviors and collisions.

The Evidence: Why Protected Lanes Work

In contrast to "paint-only" solutions, research overwhelmingly supports well-designed, physically separated lane systems. A microsimulation study on expanding the bike lane network in Downtown Atlanta found that dedicated lanes significantly improved safety by separating cyclists from vehicle traffic (Lee, 2022). Broader research across 12 U.S. cities confirms that cities with more protected bike infrastructure see significantly fewer fatalities and serious injuries for all road users, including motorists (Marshall & Ferenchak, 2019).

Efficiency & Economy

Contary to the belief that bike lanes hinder cars, Atlanta-based simulations show that a modal shift of just 5% from cars to bikes decreases average stop delays for the entire network (Lee, 2022). Furthermore, limiting micromobility has economic costs; a nighttime ban on e-scooters in Atlanta increased travel times by up to 37% during large events and cost the city millions in lost productivity (Asensio et al., 2022).

A Model for Success: Georgia Tech's Cycle Track

Georgia Tech has already demonstrated the viability of these concepts with the Tech Parkway and Ferst Drive cycle track projects. Completed between 2019 and 2025, this infrastructure created a protected east-west corridor that physically separates riders from vehicles using curbs, buffers, and concrete barriers.

This design enhances Safety by eliminating lane incursions, improves Connectivity by linking the Campus Rec Center to Tech Square, and supports Sustainability by encouraging low-carbon transit. The success of this "Gold-level" infrastructure serves as a blueprint for the adjacent city streets.

Proposed Solution for Marietta Street

We propose extending the safety standards of Tech Parkway to Marietta Street. To prevent dangerous interactions between cars and bikes, the street should be redesigned to include:

  • Raised Bike Lanes: Paving lanes flush with the sidewalk or installing bollards to physically prevent vehicle entry.
  • Bike-Specific Signals: Dedicated traffic lights to manage conflicts between turning vehicles and through-traffic riders.
  • Clear Signage: Improved regulatory signage to prevent confusion regarding parking zones and merging areas.

Budget & Implementation Options

To provide flexibility for the City, we present two implementation pathways:

Option A: Quick-Build Pilot (~$325,000)

Designed for rapid deployment to test effectiveness using flexible materials.

Item Cost
Pavement markings & striping (4,000 ft) $120,000
Flexible delineator posts (200 units) $40,000
Signal heads & Signage $57,000
Design & Contingency $110,000

Option B: Permanent Build (~$1.3 Million)

A durable, long-term transformation with concrete infrastructure.

Item Cost
Concrete curb-separated lanes (0.8 mi) $800,000
Signal heads & Signage $57,000
Design & Contingency $430,000

Conclusion

The current design of streets like Marietta Street poses serious risks to the community. Painted bike lanes are no longer enough. We urge the Atlanta Department of Transportation to implement a connected network of protected bike lanes.
